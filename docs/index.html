We highlight the contrast in Fig. A.2. Tune-A-Video (TAV) produces videos with poor temporal consistency and significant content changes (e.g., the crowd behind the racing car, the fields behind the cat). MasaCtrl*, FateZero, and TokenFlow tend to generate videos that are highly similar to the source, but fail to make edits that match the prompt (e.g., lack of raindrops in column 1 and noticeable stylistic changes in columns 2 and 3). Similarly, Rerender-A-Video also falls short in aligning with the target prompt and has significant color differences compared to the source. Additionally, all baselines are unable to modify the temporal dependency of the source video, leading to their failure in motion editing.


In contrast, the proposed UniEdit stands out for its ability to generate videos with 1) better content preservation 2) alignment with the target prompt 3) capability for various appearance editing tasks (including global stylization, local object replacement, or background modification), and motion editing.


In addition to the 20 evaluation videos from the original paper, we have expanded the validation set with 20 randomly sampled source video clips from the loveu-tgve-2023 [1] dataset, along with their corresponding 80 text prompts, resulting in a total of 100 edited videos. Observation: The query and key features (in SA-S modules) dictate the spatial structure of the generated video, while the value features tend to influence the texture, including details such as color tones.
To comprehend why we can have inhomogeneous QK and V and their differences, we visualized the results of swapping different features (QK or V) in SA-S modules during style transfer tasks on the source video in Fig. B.1. As can be seen, compared to Fig. B.1(b) with no feature replacement, replacing QK (Fig. B.1(c)) results in the edited video adopting the same spatial structure as the source video. Simultaneously, replacing V eradicates the style information in (Fig. B.1(b)), meaning the texture details from the source video are utilized to replace the style depicted by the target prompt.

Hyper-parameter Stability and Analysis. For appearance editing, we observed that altering the blend layers and steps in Eq. 4 can effectively regulate the extent to which the edited image adheres to the original image. For instance, in the stylization shown in Fig. C.1, injecting the attention map into fewer (15) steps yields a stylized output that may not retain the same structure as the input, whereas injecting into all 50 steps results in videos with nearly identical textures but less stylization. Users can adjust the blended layers and steps to achieve their preferred balance between stylization and fidelity.
For motion editing, we use content preservation in Eq. 2 to maintain the original content (e.g., textures and background) from the source video. We varied the feature injection steps and layers in Fig. C.3 & C.4. We observed that replacing only the value features at a few steps leads to inconsistencies in the background (footprints on the beach) and character ID (height of the man). We also examined the impact of motion injection in Eq.3. As depicted in Fig. C.3, injecting into a few steps fails to edit the original motion in the source video; as the injection steps increase, the motion in the edited video progressively changes from 'walk' to 'stride' to 'run'. Therefore, adjusting these parameters can be seen as a trade-off between the "similarity of the edited video to the source video" and the "magnitude of motion in the edited video". Similar trade-offs are also encountered in the field of consistent generation. For instance, a concurrent work, ConsiStory [2] (released after the submission deadline), balances identity consistency and spatial layout diversity by tweaking the blending parameter and dropout probability, which is unavoidable for tuning-free methods. It's crucial to highlight that while the results may vary with parameter changes, reasonable outputs can still be obtained, albeit with differences in "consistency with the source video" and ""the magnitude of motion". Despite the variations, the core functionality and effectiveness of the method remain intact.

To investigate the impact of mask-guided coordination, we begin by visualizing masks obtained from 1) the attention map in CA-S modules; 2) the off-the-shelf segmentation model SAM [1], followed by presenting both qualitative and quantitative results of implementing UniEdit with or without mask-guided coordination.

As verified by previous work [2], the attention maps in CA-S modules contain correspondence information between text and visual features. The underlying intuition is that the attention maps between each word and the spatial features at point (i, j) indicate 'how similar this token is to the spatial feature at this location'. We visualize the text-image cross attention map alongside the synthesized video in Section D. We observe spatial correspondences that align with the video output from the attention map. For instance, areas with higher values of the token 'man' and 'NYC' correspond to the foreground and background, respectively. We further employ a fixed threshold (0.4 in practice) to derive binary segmentation maps from the attention maps. For comparison, we also display the segmentation mask obtained by point prompt on SAM. It's observed that the cross-attention mask is generally accurate and could serve as a reliable proxy in practice when an external segmentor is not available.

We examine the impact of mask-guided coordination through both qualitative and quantitative results across 4 settings: {w/o UniEdit, UniEdit w/o mask, UniEdit with mask from CA-S, UniEdit with mask from SAM}. Qualitatively, the implementation of UniEdit significantly enhances the consistency between the edited videos and the original video. The application of the mask-guided coordination technique further improves the consistency of unedited areas (e.g., color and texture). The quantitative results above align coherently with this analysis.

We exhibit failure cases in section F. Rows 1-3 showcase when editing multiple elements simultaneously, and we observe a relatively large inconsistency with the source video.
